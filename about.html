Computational Chemistry


What is Computational Chemistry?

Computational chemistry is a set of techniques for investigating chemical problems on a computer. It is used to determine different molecular modeling related things. Computational chemistry is like having a high-tech assistant in the chemistry lab. It taps into the power of computers to help solve complex chemical puzzles. It utilizes advanced mathematical models and efficient computer programs to predict the structures and properties of molecules and solids. While it often complements experimental findings, it can also uncover new chemical phenomena that haven't been observed before. This technology is incredibly useful in fields like drug design and material science.

There are different approaches to computational chemistry. Some methods are highly accurate but are only practical for small systems. Others are less precise but can handle much larger systems, incorporating experimental data alongside theoretical principles; these are known as empirical or semi-empirical methods. Both approaches contribute to our understanding of chemical systems and drive advancements in various scientific fields.

Molecular geometry: the shapes of molecules - bond lengths, angles and dihedrals. Energies of molecules and transition states: this tells us which isomer is favored at equilibrium, and how fast a reaction should go.

Chemical reactivity: for example, knowing where the electrons are concentrated (nucleophilic sites) and where they want to go (electrophilic sites) enables us to predict where various kinds of reagents will attack a molecule. IR, UV and NMR spectra: these can be calculated, and if the molecule is unknown, someone trying to make it knows what to look for.

The interaction of a substrate with an enzyme: seeing how a molecule fits into the active site of an enzyme is one approach to designing better drugs.

The physical properties of substances: these depend on the properties of individual molecules and on how the molecules interact in the bulk material. For example, the strength and melting point of a polymer (e.g. a plastic) depend on how well the molecules fit together and on how strong the forces between them are. People who investigate things like this work in the field of materials science.


Famous Computational Chemists

Jacobus Henricus van 't Hoff

Van 't Hoff formulated the theory of the tetrahedral carbon atom and laid the foundations of stereochemistry. He predicted the correct structures of allenes and cumulenes as well as their axial chirality.

Kenichi Fukui

Fukui shared his frontier orbital theory of chemical reactivity with Hoffmann who independently developed similar theories.
